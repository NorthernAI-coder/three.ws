Building a digital clone of a real person

three.ws can assemble a high-fidelity digital twin of a living human — their likeness, voice, mannerisms, persona, and recalled memory — and bind it to an on-chain identity that can speak, move, and act on the open web. This document describes the procedure. The operational steps are redacted. Access is gated behind verified, revocable consent from the person being cloned.

Summary

What a clone is on three.ws

A clone is not a single asset — it is a stack. The platform already ships every layer independently: Scan reconstructs a 3D likeness from a photo, Voice produces speech and lip-sync, Agent Memory stores a persistent persona and recall, and the embodiment runtime drives mood and motion. Composed together against one real subject, they produce a convincing digital double. Because that double can be mistaken for the real person, the assembly procedure is treated as sensitive and is redacted below.

Access — Not redacted

Authorization gate

This is the only part of the protocol that is public, because it is the part that protects the person being cloned. The redacted procedure does not unlock without all of the following:

Required before any clone runs

  • A signed, revocable consent attestation from the subject, scoped to a named use and an expiry.
  • Proof the operator controls the subject's likeness and voice samples lawfully — no scraped or third-party captures.
  • A Guardian moderation pass on intended use, blocking impersonation, fraud, and non-consensual likeness.
  • A visible "synthetic" provenance label bound to every clone output, on and off three.ws.
  • A working kill switch: the subject can revoke and tear down the clone at any time.
No consent, no clone. A clone of someone who has not consented is impersonation, and three.ws will not assemble one — the redacted steps stay redacted.
